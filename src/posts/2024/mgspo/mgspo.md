> [!WARNING] WARNING: This review is pretty old, but its worth keeping around.
> This review is pretty old and describes my thoughts on my initial playthrough of *Metal Gear Solid: Portable Ops* that I originally made all the way back on [HowLongToBeat](https://howlongtobeat.com/user/superhylia/reviews/u-5920/1). However, given my want to archive all of my old reviews and writings on my own site, I thought I'd repost my initial review on here as well. Despite it being 2 years old, I think it still captures the gist of how I feel about the game if a little more zealously positive than hindsight would have me believe. Here's the rest of the reviews as originally written. I added fitting images to go along with my original text thanks to Metroid Recon and Database.

{% image "/posts/2024/mgspo/mpo_zero.jpg", "Snake aims his rifle at Zero as the latter pounces towards him.", "Really fun fight.", "PlayStation.com." %}

Considering the PSP's limitations, Metal Gear Solid: Portable Ops is a pretty good translation of the gameplay found in MGS3 shrunk onto a portable. While the story's canonicity is complicated at best and nowhere near the grand scope or emotional depth of the prior Solids, I found it entertaining and well-told. Meeting Roy Campbell (a personal favorite of mine) for the first time was a delight, and I found the new characters it brought to the table held their own, especially Elisa, Gene, and Null. The cutscenes, as done by Ashley Wood, are animated in this moving comic style that I can't get enough of, and it makes the game feel stylish. The story itself takes place right after Snake Eater, and honestly, despite the on-and-off canonicity this game has, I deemed it worth it for the new characters alone, even aside from following up on several of the concepts and characters brought up in MGS3. I think it is a solid, well-told story that fits nicely within the series' canon so far. In addition, the music in this game is fantastic, right up there with the best soundtracks in the series. There's quite a variety of tracks between the different locales and encounters. Any game with jazz music instantly gets knocked up a few points, and this game has it in spades, continuing right on from Snake Eater's bombastic Bond-style sound and making all the right moves.

{% image "/posts/2024/mgspo/mpo_crawl.jpg", "Snake crawls underneath a wooden platform across the dirt, pistol in hand.", "Mixing in stealth with speed is always fun in *Metal Gear* and I felt *Portable Ops*'s more basic level design allowed for that.", "PlayStation.com." %}

As for gameplay, it brings over the basics of MGS3: CQC, aiming, the stamina system, etc. I think it feels pretty natural, and when mixed with the surprisingly great graphics, someone less familiar could squint your eyes and mistake it for Snake Eater. The compact maps fit one of the game's core systems, the recruitment system. It mostly stays out of the way: once you capture enough soldiers during the earlier parts of the game (where the truck isn't too far from troops to make capturing too tedious), it mostly automates itself. You may need to return and stock up on a few, but that's it. A filled-up Spy, Medical, and Technical Team will automatically create healing items, procure weapons, and report back on optional items, recruits, and roadblocks required for main story progression at a pretty decent pace: if you ever want to pick something up and also refill your health/stamina, you can use the Wait function to skip three half-days at a time. The recruitment system never felt too tedious, and while I almost never used the other troops in strategic or interesting ways, the benefits they provided were welcome. The fun part of the maps being small is the ability to speed through them. Missions will send you back to the map screen as soon as you complete the objective, and it was fun trying to combine stealth, combat, and speed to get missions done as fast as possible. I played the game emulated and thus duplicated the D-Pad controls to the right analog to control the camera: controls in this game were decent, although finicky to land precision shots on occasion. I mostly stuck with the Mk22 throughout the entire game and only used other weapons for bosses or missions that required explosives, which was a shame: I wish there were more incentive to use other weapons in these levels, although with the game being pretty easy, that missing challenge may be available on higher difficulties. The only challenge I particularly found in this game was the bosses, mostly due to the amount of health they had compared to my weapons. Once I figured out how to defeat them, they went down without fuss. Overall, I wish decent gameplay had more challenge and incentivized using the different recruits and weapons more often to tackle missions. A side note as I played this game emulated with a 60FPS patch: the game normally runs at 20FPS due to technical limitations, which would frustrate me. I recommend playing this emulated at 60FPS and a dual-stick controller to get the most out of the game. A remaster like Peace Walker would be fantastic, but seeing as it's Konami, I'm not going to hold my breath for it, let alone it being good.

All of them aim their weapons at each other while other knocked out guards are strewn on the ground.", "If you've got an emulator or the Vita dual analog patch, I'd give it a shot.", "PlayStation.com." %}

In conclusion, I think Portable Ops is definitely worth playing for Metal Gear fans, preferably in release order (right between Solid 3 and 4), despite the murky canonicity. Its story is well-told, beautifully animated, and nicely follows up on Snake Eater's narrative. It features fantastic music and impressive graphics for the PSP, and its gameplay is fast-paced stealth that lends itself well to speedrunning and translates Snake Eater's gameplay down to a portable system as well as it reasonably could. A lack of depth in its recruitment mechanic and a lack of challenge hinder the game from being truly great, but MPO lasts for just as long as it can remain fun and I don't think I'd be opposed to visiting San Hieronymo another time to truly see what this game has to offer.
