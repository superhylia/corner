Recently, I've been on a pretty big [personal project to examine 30 NES/Famicom games](https://superhylia.dev/blog/introducing-new-gaze-the-nes/famicom-and-the-weight-of-age/) to try to see if they hold up today and whether that exploration can bring up some conversations about how we value games as art, if remakes or future successors devalue their 8-bit predecessors, and our willingness to appreciate age. One of the games I'm planning to cover as a part of that series is Sweet Home, a 1989 Famicom adaptation of the Kiyoshi Kurosawa and Juzo Itami film that laid the groundwork for the survival horror genre, including inspiring the very game I talk about today. While my playthrough of Resident Evil (2002 Remake) wasn't necessarily inspired by this endeavor, I started it months ago and have been playing on and off since; perhaps I've finished it just in time for it to resonate with me most. Like its 2004 Metroid counterpart, this game's reputation as a remake precedes it and, for my generation sans influence, has arguably superseded the original in all forms: in canonicity, in conversation, in availability, and most relevant for us, in recommendation.

As time goes on, I wonder if there's a chilling effect in that a future game supplants what used to be there, especially for one of the most influential games ever made and the start of one of the biggest franchises the medium will ever see. Part of me longs for the experience as it was, and I plan to play the original and Chris' campaign of the remake as part of the discussion of Sweet Home, both to see how the original Resident Evil respects its ancestor and if the remake lives up to all the potential it could've had for me. Make no mistake, though: if the original vision for RE1 from director Tokuro Fujiwara was to create a suspenseful game that could define survival horror as a genre in itself, then REmake accomplishes this with frightening class, not just for the classic it reimagines but as one of the best games I'll ever play on its own.

The disc spins and a cutscene plays. After a slew of mysterious murders in Racoon City, both the Alpha and Bravo divisions of the Special Tactics and Rescue Service team (S.T.A.R.S.) are on the case. Two teams turn into one after a helicopter crash and after an intense encounter with bloodthirsty dogs, four become three. The surviving members of Alpha Team, Captain Wesker, Barry, and our player character, Jill Valentine, run into the Spencer Mansion for mistaken refuge. While the CG intro is well paced, only once the doors open to reveal the inside of the mansion that Resident Evil reveals one of its best qualities: its visuals.

While my playthrough was via the HD Remaster, this game looks phenomenal and stands as my vote for the best-looking game released on the GameCube, surpassing even RE4 or my beloved Metroid Prime duology. Every camera angle, every cast of the impressive lighting, and every particle effect or animated environment inculcates the atmosphere of the game. That atmosphere is also in no small part thanks to the ambient soundtrack that accompanies the full range of emotions the game employs; even if cliché to call it my favorite track, the sounds of safety during this game’s rendition of the Save Room theme aren’t entirely consoling, as if the terror of what lies outside still remains. As the penultimate entry in the series to take advantage of fixed camera angles and pre-rendered backgrounds, the format is executed with incredible justice.

The acting and story presentation are definitely less intimidating, but it's charming in its own way. Despite the remake taking great pains to instill a more frightening atmosphere into the player and absolutely succeeding, the story is the same B-movie-esque plot as it ever was, with the acting to match. Some of the lines are so... cheesy, like the writers are winking at the players, hinting that the 1996 game, with its lovable but stilted voice acting, is still beneath and very much alive. Everyone is likable, from Barry to Brad, even if my girl Jill can be pretty unaware at times. However, underneath the cheese lies a compelling tale of corporate corruption, scientific sacrifice, and the way humans are pushed to their emotional and physical bounds by the mutations Umbrella deploys. Admittedly, while the game is not very “scary” outside a few jump scares here and there, there’s enough here that I would describe the game as “haunting”. That sensation is communicated brilliantly through the several notes and journals you encounter throughout the game, with my favorite featuring Lisa Trevor, who was newly added to the remake. The game uses her journal to convey her slow descent after Umbrella administers the Progenitor virus extremely well, making the latter boss encounter with her more compelling.

Speaking of bosses, that brings us to the gameplay, which has been a fairly contentious topic in survival horror games for as long as the genre has existed. Since the beginning, horror games have striven to immerse the player in an environment that is absent and defiant of the familiar, and as part of that experience, their mechanics and core gameplay loops are designed around that philosophy. In that intention is a conflict: should horror games be *fun* to play, and if not fun, what *should* playing them feel like? What if that game feel, in its attempt to be precarious or unusual relative to the unsettling scenario the game takes place in, isn't typical or even seen as “good” by the accepted standards of contemporary games? A descriptor for gameplay appeal I've seen used for games in this genre, both in romanticization and in criticism, is "bad," "a relic of its time," or "clunky," which to me feels reductive and a subsidization of what makes games in this genre and many others with non-standard, non-tactile gameplay so enticing. From my perspective, good gameplay design should serve and amplify the artists' intended experience (yes, all people on the dev team are artists): what they want you to feel, how they want you to interact with the world, and how that world, in turn, encourages decision-making. Thankfully, that’s what *Resident Evil* is best at.

The core gameplay loop of *REmake* is as follows: Jill (or Chris) explore the Spencer Mansion, an interconnected map with progression continually gated by keys/other inventory items found through exploration and puzzles, all while balancing the risk-reward of engaging in combat and balancing your limited health and inventory filled with resources like Ink Ribbons to save, Herbs to heal, and ammo for weapons. If it sounds kind of familiar, it should be! While I wouldn’t say I entirely agree with *RE1* being a Metroidvania (nomenclature [Capcom developers have used to describe these games and their reliance on backtracking](https://variety.com/2018/gaming/features/resident-evil-2-interview-1202848886/)), I would say it mostly fulfills my genre definition of an interconnected map featuring ability-gated exploration. The key difference between the two (pun intended) is that the Mansion is gated by *limited-use* keys that will be discarded once all doors that require them have been opened, rather than gated by *multi-use* abilities like the Speed Booster in Metroid that also enhance combat or traversal capabilities. Thankfully, on its own merits, the map is well-structured and well-presented in-game, sending you through many floors of the Spencer Mansion, the Courtyard, the outdoor Residence, the Altar, and the underground Laboratory. What rooms you check, what hallways you’ll take, when you’ll use a Typewriter or stave off an Ink Ribbon save at the Typewriters for the next chunk of your objectives became more and more of a conscious decision as the game progressed.

Inventory management became a minigame of its own and is the heart of the “survival” in survival horror. Resources for your inventory range from ammo, weapons/self-defense items, saves via Ink Ribbons, health/recovery, and items for progression/utility. Jill is technically the “easier” campaign, featuring eight inventory slots compared to Chris’ six, earlier access to the Assault Shotgun, access to the Grenade Launcher rather than the Flamethrower, and a lockpick as her special starter item at the cost of less health and swapping Barry for sometimes-playable Rebecca (who would later star in 2002’s *RE0*). Still, though, not being able to drop items at any time means the player must make conscious decisions at all times for the items they decide to take with them and hope they’ll survive until the relatively underpopulated Item Boxes to drop them off. This is also encouraged by the wonderful herb health system: found in three varieties, green (for health) and blue (for poison) can be taken alone or combined, with red herbs further amplifying their effects when mixed for more effective healing. There are many stretches of the game where I found myself scrounging for ammo or risking low health to haul some good loot back, or deciding whether to use a single Green Herb or wait until I got back to the Item Box to mix it with a Red Herb for a fuller heal later. 

Items are one area where the remake distinguishes itself mechanically, introducing self-defense items and the Fuel Canteen to burn zombie corpses. Defense Items saved my ass so many times, and while Jill doesn’t have the instant-kill flash grenades in Chris’s playthrough, the stun gun and batteries she does have really came through on many occasions when zombies grab you quickly. As for burning corpses, I didn’t take advantage of burning enemies as much as I really should have in hindsight. By filling up the Fuel Canteen with kerosene tanks scattered throughout the map, you can pour and then use the Lighter (Chris’ special starter item) to burn their corpses and prevent them from reviving. Without this, all zombies with intact heads/limbs will eventually turn into extremely quick, tanky Crimson Heads, which do a lot more damage and will chase you the moment you enter the area, no matter the distance. One of them stalked the frequently travelled mirror corridor throughout the game, while another appeared in the staircase to the third floor in the Laboratory, making every repeat trip very panicked. 

Ammo was just the right balance of infrequent enough along the main path that every shot could very easily snowball into a bigger problem later, but in enough supply scattered to different rooms that it was often worth exploring. Bosses weren’t too difficult but were engaging enough that utilizing your stronger weapons for these special occasions, rather than hoarding them, felt worthwhile. My favorite encounters were the ones that employed alternative solutions, like Plant 42 via a dose of T-JOLT, or were entirely skippable with good routing, which tied these encounters back into the core game loop. Saving as a resource is a hallmark of the genre that the 1996 game birthed, and while there’s certainly enough for a normal playthrough if a little cautious, it did make for an extremely tense session where I found myself out of Ink Ribbons within the Mansion right before Plant 42 (don’t ask, I had to save a lot due to daily play sessions). While having to survive Yawn, grabbing the gemstones and M.O. Disks, and Black Tiger to make it to the Altar, finally letting out a sigh of relief, was a highlight of the game in terms of tension, it also made a great demonstration of the value routing throughout the map, one that made me wish I relied on guides a little less to ensure the next objectives.

Like the photograph within a locket, what frames the inventory management is what many see as a noose around the necks of this game and its sequels, and perhaps the perfect example of *Resident Evil* brilliantly designing for horror: the tank controls. On the one hand, I have seen that this game (remake or original), or other survival horror games that employ fixed camera angles and tank controls, can center their identities on these mechanics at the expense of everything else the games do well. On the other hand, I personally believe that controls *are* gameplay; they are not just an intermediary form of communication between the player and the game, but a vital part of expressing a game's design kinesthetically. This isn’t to say there’s no value in multiple control schemes that may be more streamlined, nor that they're without fault or aren’t also products of their time, but I strongly believe that controls in older games aren’t the mile-high barrier they’re perceived to be. They are contextualized within the game’s design and the year they were released, and are only made a source of friction to engage with by the passage of time, rather than being inherently flawed systems.

In early 3D games, before free-roaming cameras became prevalent, tank controls were introduced as just one of many approaches to player movement. A control scheme popularized by *Resident Evil* and *Tomb Raider* (both 1996: great year for revolutionary games, it seems), the player controls the player character/protagonist's movement relative to the direction they face rather than the perspective of a camera. If the character is facing forward, the player will press in that direction to go forward. If they want to turn them, they'll press toward the character's back. Turning left and right rotates the character’s front (like a tank!) in preparation to move forward by pressing up again. For convenience's sake, continuing to press keeps the character moving in that direction, even when many survival horror games of the time shift or pan angles. By contrast, current games, or as a contemporary example from 1996 video games, in something like *Super Mario 64*, the character would strafe left/right or “simultaneously” turn and move in the direction you press. It’s important to note that while tank controls arose from the limitations of early 3D, I cannot overstate how much and how quickly game design would change within the fifth generation, only for more modern schemes to become standard by sixth-gen systems.

While there definitely is a learning curve, one I would recommend embarking on over using the alternate/”modern” control mode the *HD Remaster* provides, the one-two punch of the controls and the fixed camera angles truly tie the inventory management, the encounters with Umbrella’s experiments, and the atmosphere of the interconnected map together into one cohesive masterpiece. Every enemy, from the regular zombies spread throughout the game to the Hunters and Chimera that become prevalent as you progress, is balanced around your stilted mobility. You will get captured at some point. You will end up stumbling backwards into the wrong room or into a new camera angle when trying to back away from an enemy. You will find yourself having that extra second of thought when trying to maneuver. The controls empower the mutated beasts that surround the Arklay Mountains against you, but despite not being able to suddenly run faster or jump higher, practice becomes your greatest teacher. You learn to sneak past zombies by modulating speed and making quick use of rotation to squeeze by. You learn to reckon with quick 180° turns for faster retreat. You come to rely on, perhaps revel in, the consistency of controls; angle changes may disorient at first, but where you move and where you face is never in question. Aim. Recede. Shoot. Mix. Run. Turn. Heal. Save. On the other side of friction is the empowerment of your own, becoming a master of a mansion made to scare that comes to fear you instead.

As I grinned seeing Wesker’s yellow hairdo dye itself crimson into a blood red pool stabbed by his own creation, Tyrant, and regrouped with Chris and Barry, I began to formulate my thoughts on how I’d felt about the game so far as it came to an end. Expectations can impact how you perceive a game, especially when expectations are so high in the case of this game. Did it live up to them? What do I like about this game? Is it love? Two attempts at fighting Tyrant on the helipad and one blown-up mansion later, I knew it was something I hadn’t captured before but saw many times in the past in different forms and different games. It was birth.

Yes, it is birth in the sense that it's the first game (canonically) of a franchise that’s known for reinventing itself to let things be new again, even if sometimes it's at the expense of what used to be there. In Capcom’s opinion, [maybe loss is fine if what remains is superior](https://www.thegamebusiness.com/p/when-we-launched-resident-evil-on): to their eyes, a 75% batting average for remakes in this series is hard to argue with, enough that people want more. I’m writing this at a time when *Resident Evil*, not the 2002 game, but the original game, is rumored to be remade for the second time, almost guaranteed to carry over the third-person camera and movement *RE4* and its successors ran away with. Not many games get the honor of a second remake: going off my experience with *Pokémon Let’s Go*, maybe that *isn’t* an honor. For the game that birthed that demand and the standards that many future remakes of other classics still fail to reach, where does this game stand on a day when its standing as what *Resident Evil 1* is to most people may be challenged soon? Should it have had that standing in the first place? 

*Resident Evil* (2002) is the birth of twins, Tragedy and Fortune. Tragedy is heralded as a replacement for a game it held in such high regard, enough respect that it taught me why survival horror is as compelling as it is. Made with such love and admiration for the original that it's still underneath, under a cheesy veneer perhaps, but wholly ambitious just as it was because it understood what was frightening. A new remake may come out. Maybe it'll be good and take risks in its own way, but the consequences of the belief that one game can replace another will show themselves again for the second time: a ship of Theseus made manifest for one of gaming’s most influential. What becomes of *RE1* and how we talk about it? Fortune lives her life though. She signs her fair share of autographs, but there’s always a worry that age is getting to her. It takes a while to get to know her and she lives pretty far out, but she isn’t too worried. She wears itchy sweaters, but with a tasty cup of hot chocolate in hand, she tells the best ghost story you’ve heard in a very long time. It's one you’ve probably heard before, but it always strikes you like lightning, like you were there the first time it was told. What power makes men do, what power can force onto others, and what power good people obtain for themselves. Fortune favors the bold, and she would know, after all. Fear can’t kill you, especially if that fear is re-entering survival horror’s genesis, but, remade, a story may take a crimson form. Different, sure, but alive again, red heart still beating, with all the love in the world to stay alive as one of the medium's bloody best.
