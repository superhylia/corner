Recently, I've been on a pretty big [personal project to examine 30 NES/Famicom games](https://superhylia.dev/blog/introducing-new-gaze-the-nes/famicom-and-the-weight-of-age/) to try to see if they hold up today and whether that exploration can bring up some conversations about how we value games as art, if remakes or future successors de-value their 8-bit predecessors, and our willingness to appreciate age. One of the games I'm planning to cover as apart of that series is *Sweet Home*, a 1989 Famicom adaptation of the Kiyohshi Kurosawa and Juzo Itami film that laid the groundwork for the survival horror genre, including inspiring the very game I talk about today. While my playthrough of ***Resident Evil* (2002 Remake)** wasn't necessarily inspired by this endeavor, I started it months ago and have been playing on and off since, perhaps I've finished it just in time for it to resonate with me most. Like its 2004 *Metroid* counterpart, this game's reputation as a remake precedes it and for my generation sans influence has arguably superseded the original in all forms: in canonicity, in conversation, in availability, and most relevant for us, in recommendation.

As time goes on, I wonder if there's a chilling effect in that, a future game supplanted in the stead of what used to be there, especially for one of the most influential games ever made and the start of one of the biggest franchises the medium will ever see. Part of me longs for the experience as it was and I plan to play the original and Chris' campaign of the remake as part of the discussion of *Sweet Home*, both to see how the original *Resident Evil* respects its ancestor and if the remake lives up to all the potential it could've had for myself. Make no mistake, though: if the original vision for *RE1* from director Tokuro Fujiwara from was to create a suspenseful game that could define survival horror as a genre in itself, then *REmake* accomplishes this with frightening class, not just for the classic it reimagines but as one of the best games I'll ever play on its own.

The disc spins and a cutscene plays. After a slew of mysterious murders in Racoon City, both the Alpha and Bravo divisions of the Special Tactics and Rescue Service team (S.T.A.R.S.) are on the case. Two teams turn into one after a helicopter crash and with an intense encounter with blood-thirsty dogs, four become three. The surviving members of Alpha Team, Captain Wesker, Barry, and our player character, Jill Valentine, run into the Spencer Mansion for mistaken repreive. While the CG intro is well paced, only once the doors open to reveal the inside of the mansion does *Resident Evil* reveal one of its best qualities: its visuals.

While my playthrough was via the HD Remaster, this game looks *phenomenal* and stands as my vote for the best-looking game released on the GameCube, surpassing even *RE4* or my beloved *Metroid Prime* duology. Every camera angle, every cast of the impressive lighting, and every particle effect or animated environment inculcates the atmosphere of the game. As the penultimate entry in the series to take advantage of fixed camera angles and pre-rendered backgrounds, the format is executed with incredible justice.

The acting and story presentation is definitely less intimidating, but its charming within its own way. Despite the remake taking great pains to instill a more fearful environment into the player and absolutely succeeding, the story is the same B-movie-esque plot as it ever was with the acting to match. Some of the lines are so...cheesy, like the writers are winking to the players that the 1996 game with its lovable but stilted voice acting is still underneath and very much alive. Everyone is likeable from Barry to Brad, even if my girl Jill can be pretty unaware at times. However, underneath the cheese is a compelling tale of corporate corruption, scientific sacrifice, and the limits humanity will go to 

This is communicated brilliantly with the several notes and journals you encounter throughout the game

brings us to gameplay, which has been a decently contentious topic for survival horror games as a genre for as long as they've been prevalent. Since the beginning, horror games have strived to immerse the player in an environment that is absent of the familiar or at least defiant of typical expectations in order and as apart of that experience, their mechanics and core gameplay loops are approached with that philosophy. In that intention is a conflict: should horror games be *fun* to play and if not fun, what *should* playing them feel like? What if that game feel, in its attempt to be precarious or unusual relative to the unsettling scenario the game takes place in, isn't typical or even seen as good by the standards of other games? A descriptor for gameplay appeal I've seen used for games in this genre, both in romanticization and in criticism is "bad," "a relic of its time," or "clunky," which to me feels reductive and a subsidization of what makes games in this genre and many others with non-standard, non-tactile gameplay so enticing. From my perspective, good gameplay design should serve and amplify the intended experience of the artists (yes, all people on the dev team are artists): what they want you to feel, how they want you to interact with the world, and how that world encourages decision making in turn. What many see as a noose around this game and its sequels neck is the perfect example of *Resident Evil* brilliantly designing for horror: the tank controls.

In early 3D games before free-roaming cameras became prevalent, tank controls were introduced as just one of many approaches to player movement. A control scheme popularized by *Resident Evil* and *Tomb Raider* (both 1996: great year for revolutionary games, it seems), the player controls the player character/protagonists movement **relative to the direction they face** rather than the perspective of a camera. If the character is facing forward, the player will press in that direction to go forward. If they want to turn them, they'll press toward the characters back. Turning left and right will rotate the character (like a tank!) rather than strafe as is standard in current games. For conveniences sake, continuing to press would maintain the characters movement in that direction even when the angle shifted like in many survival horror games of the time.
