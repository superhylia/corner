## Metal Gear
{% image "/posts/2023/mg12-1st/mg1_card.jpg", "Snake picks up Card 1 inside the cargo of a trailer truck.", "Finding these things was hell.", "Konami for the Master Collection." %}

Music is good, and I like how each weapon and item is used at least once throughout the game, but that's the most I can say. Very bland; the stealth is more like waiting around rather than being able to actually use the environment to your advantage, and several cheap traps and surprise enemies at the end only make things worse.

## Metal Gear 2: Solid Snake
For a 1990 8-bit MSX game, *Metal Gear 2: Solid Snake* has impressive music and graphics even by the standards of other 8-bit games and is a world of improvement on the original game. The gameplay was far more engaging with vastly improved stealth, better map design, more multi-use items (although you never really need to use non-Handgun weapons outside of bosses that require it), better bosses, and overall more challenge. However, the game falters in a few moments, which derails the experience. There is far more backtracking than in the first game, which was already annoying due to the card system. The card system is improved by giving you Red, Blue, and Green Cards to replace the individual cards for 1-3, 4-6, and 7-9, respectively, but we aren't at the point of future games where a single card will work on the lower floors as well. The game still lacks direction, and some moments, such as the bathroom stall segment, would be very hard to figure out without [a walkthrough or guide](https://lparchive.org/Metal-Gear-1-2/) (how would I know I needed to start a codec call before being able to trigger the guard?).

{% image "/posts/2023/mg12-1st/mg2_bushes.jpg", "Snake hides in the maze-like bushes from a baret-wearing guard.", "Quite a good looking game for 1990, especially the UI.", "Konami for the Master Collection." %}

The story of *MG2* is far more prevalent than the original, and while it doesn't expel on the theme of children in war it establishes, as well as still being simple and not very significant to future games, it's definitely the start of the cinematic stories future games would employ. Snake's interactions with Holly and Natasha are weird but wholesome, codec calls are vastly improved, and seeing Snake interact with Big Boss and Grey Fox before the events of future games is neat. The final part of the game reminded me a lot of MGS1's ending with Liquid Snake, and to be honest, the best way to describe *Metal Gear 2* is as a de-make of *Metal Gear Solid*. A lot of the gameplay introduced and improved on in MG2 became the foundation of Metal Gear Solid, both games introduce the kind of person Solid Snake is and why he's so interesting (although MGS does this with more runtime and voice acting), and several moments of the game are directly used in Metal Gear Solid. As I played that game and its first 2 sequels before playing the MSX duology, it was cool to notice details of future games called back to or directly reused. I especially appreciate playing the version of the game introduced in *Metal Gear Solid 3: Subsistence*, which redid the portraits to resemble the art style seen in future games, which was a really good touch that made the game feel more in-line with codec calls seen in future games.

{% image "/posts/2023/mg12-1st/mg2_map.jpg", "Snake approaches two red baret-wearing guards in a grey industrial area. The radar of guards in the area is shown in the top right side of the screen.", "Having a radar was so useful and *Metal Gear 1* could've really benefitted from something like this.", "Konami for the Master Collection." %}


Overall, for an 8-bit game, Metal Gear 2: Solid Snake is among the better ones I've played, especially from a visual and auditory standpoint in which MG2 is easily the best I've experienced thus far. However, it still feels like the blueprint for future games, and with its biggest flaw being the lack of guidance, it still feels like the times rather than something that defies it.
