> This review is from last year and describes my thoughts on my initial playthroughs of *Super Castlevania IV* on [Backloggd](https://bckl.gg/6x0). I quite like this game and would definitely write a newer review when I replay this game, but I thought I'd repost it so all my older writings with decent effort are archived. I added images to go along with the original text as written below.

For only my second "classic" Castlevania game after the original, funnily enough, this game is a great step forward for the series and feels like a great reimagining-remake-whatever of the original *Castlevania*.

It's a solid action game with really impressive sprites and effects for what's a launch-year SNES game. The music, as always with Castlevania games, is immaculate, and *IV* is one of the best-sounding games on the system by far. This game elevates the original setting of a haunted castle with great detail and an atmosphere that feels lightyears beyond what the prior NES and Game Boy games could accomplish while remaining a strong evolution from them. Treasure chests pop open and close when you jump on them and drop coins. The whip will smoothly jangle like keys when attack is held in a direction. Backgrounds cast shadows or move landscapes against the player's befitting of Dracula's treacherous keep that always makes you feel like you're sinking deeper and deeper.

{% image "/posts/2025/scv4-1st-play/bridge.png", "Simon avoids falling into the pit revealed by a crumbling bridge as bats chase behind him.", "One of my favorite backgrounds in the game, just super pretty.", "Rik Hideto." %}

Getting into what this game is known for: the 8-way whip. The iconic whip deserves its reputation and is not only animation-wise impressive but also just fun. While the strength of the whip does devalue the use of sub-weapons compared to the first game, I was still able to use most of them against bosses or annoying enemies that were either flying or long-range. I do feel at times that the level design, while decent, was not as well equipped for how strong 8-way whipping can be. Enemies hiding behind walls or directly below or above platforms have no challenge beyond the rare late-game flying eye enemy that will shoot projectiles below you as you'll always be able to defeat them. I did enjoy the swinging mechanic quite a bit, as it made the do-or-die platforming feel a bit more dynamic.

{% image "/posts/2025/scv4-1st-play/swinging.png", "Simon uses the versitile whip to swing on poes through waterfalls.", "I might have a thing for grappling hooks..", "Mobygamesisreanimated." %}

There were several frustrating moments with enemies knocking you into pits, Simon's limited mid-air mobility, and bosses with unreadable patterns for first-time players. Many parts of the game essentially guarantee death, and while, luckily, most players using the *Anniversary Collection* have save states, it is a relic of older arcade-esque trial-and-error difficulty design that hasn't aged the best. Thankfully, once you can progress, the stages aren't too long and dying is only a level restart and an annoying password of time taken. The 11 stages are varied, with every stage feeling logical as a continued passage into the castle and having one gimmick or setpiece that makes them quite memorable: the rotating cylinder sequence, the swinging chandelier, the hell-evator of swinging poles, and the gauntlet of bosses accompanied by the casting of the moonlight on the way to Dracula still stick with me after finishing the game.

{% image "/posts/2025/scv4-1st-play/3d_tube.png", "Simon platforms through a rotating 3D tube.", "Stil looks and plays phenomenally for a launch year SNES game.", "Mobygamesisreanimated." %}

Overall, *Super Castlevania IV* is a damn good action game for its time and on the system, and while I still have more Castlevania games to play, I can see this pretty easily contending for the best of the non-search-action/Metroidvania titles. Its difficulty design hasn't aged gracefully with do-or-die platforming and combat mixed with level design that occasionally is not built well for how strong the whip is. However, it has fantastic music deserving of its series title, memorable and dynamic levels, great atmosphere, and an exemplary technical showcase in both animation and detail for the system in only its first year on the market. At about 4 hours, the game is just the right length to remain interesting and most of all, fun.
