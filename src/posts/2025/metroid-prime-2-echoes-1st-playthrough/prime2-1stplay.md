> This review is pretty old and my opinions on *Metroid Prime 2: Echoes* in retrospect have improved quite a bit to a point that I don't think this review really captures. I still prefer *Prime 1* personally, but its only by a few hairs. My next playthrough will pretty assuredly be more positive on the game, but I still thought to repost my [Backloggd review](https://backloggd.com/u/superhylia/review/2402802/) so that all of my long-form reviews on that site reside on my own as well. Here's the rest of the review as originally written. I added fitting images to go along with my original text thanks to Metroid Recon and Database.

*Metroid Prime 2: Echoes* is a great example of "if it ain't broke, don't fix it." In many ways, it improves the 3D Metroid experience and calls back to past entries (particularly *Super*) to do so, while in others, the ripples of its shortened development period (to put it kindly) can be felt across the game, ultimately leaving a game with incredible potential that never fully coalesces. 

The story is far more interesting than most other games in the series. Aether has a lot of effort behind it, and the introduction of logs from fallen Troopers or bits of Luminoth lore around the world ties into this game's sublime atmosphere. The contrast between the Light and Dark worlds is strong; water flows and plants breathe in one, while storms rage and thunder drones in the other. The music is one of the best in the series and contributes to this atmosphere; experimenting with lower tones and varying samples for *Prime's* unique synth sound, plus throwing in remixes from prior games for good measure, sounds lovely. If there were a word to describe *Echoes*, it would be enchanting. Playing this game in a dark room, headphones on, and controller in hand encapsulates why this series is so special.

On the gameplay front, it's the same as the original at its core. Go back and forth exploring three-dimensional maps for expansions and shortcuts underneath ability-locked gates that expand the world more. It takes the core tenets of the Metroidvania/"search-action" genre into 3D very well, and I wish there were more 3D games in this genre to compare to and enjoy. Even if Sanctuary Fortress is a series stand-out, maps are less varied environmentally than *Prime*'s many biomes, but the design fundamentally holding them up has seen lots of improvement. New abilities allow for more interesting interconnected pathways through Aether, while old abilities (notably the Morph Ball and its upgrades) see far more varied use in combat and puzzles. The increase in Morph Ball use especially made me wish the 2D games took lessons from *Echoes* and made Samus' arsenal use feel more balanced as rooms felt more interactive. In terms of full completion, I did the whole lot for items, which wasn't too tricky regarding routing, and the better map design allowed for more interesting puzzles to find expansions and, thankfully, better previewing of where expansions lie. It strikes the golden balance of memorable locales and easy traversal, making item completion approachable to all players. The abilities from *Prime* are the same as ever, but the new skills and visors belie both *Echoes* strongest draw and biggest missed opportunity.

Some abilities, like the Seeker Missile, return in later games and feel appropriate here. The most significant core action change is the introduction of the Light and Dark Beams, replacing the Plasma and Ice Beams, respectively. These beams utilize Ammo that is gained by using the opposing Beam on enemies, an inspired introduction to resource management that I enjoyed. This and the continual damage taken in Dark Aether (lessened and negated by the Dark and Light Suit later in the game) made Dark Aether feel extremely oppressive and dangerous initially but gradually become more accommodating. I felt that in the original *Prime*, due to its slower pace and lack of mobility-focused abilities, the drastic progression we see from Samus was lessened compared to, say *Zero Mission*, where she controls fundamentally differently by the game's end. The toxic damage of Dark Aether and the return of the classic Screw Attack and accompanying wall jump countered this complaint, and I really enjoyed Samus' evolution throughout the game. 

However, compared to *Prime's* arsenal of situationally useful Visors, *Echoes* implementation of the mechanic left me disappointed. They aren't of real use in this game, and while there is the rare sonic-based device or invisible platform outside of one enemy type that makes good use of the Dark Visor, the varied use of Visors in *Prime* that I enjoyed so much felt ripped away when the mechanic could've had potential. The contrast between Light and Dark World progressively lessens as the game continues and Samus unlocks Suits, which disappointed me. The contrast between both of them left a strong impression on Dark Aether, and the uncommon half-dimensional object or back-and-forth puzzle made me wish the interaction between the worlds was more of a focal point, as by the end of the game, the Dark variants of maps ended up feeling desolate by boredom, not atmosphere, as the challenge slowly subsided.

Speaking of challenge, that would certainly be an understatement for the bosses. While *Prime* featured puzzle-focused bosses, which mostly worked to its benefit, *Echoes* incorporates more combat and challenge to...mixed results. I love the idea of the Guardians reflecting the abilities Samus is about to gain, and it is the perfect way to silently teach the player in a very subtle way that feels right for this franchise. Many of them, like Quadraxis, are highlights for the series and were designed excellently. That being said, the experience of defeating the bosses, some of whom are infamously difficult, was mediocre. Many of them are tanky, fast, have small or short targets, and are non-communicative about how to defeat them, even with the Scans fully read, which made some of them very frustrating to deal with. The game overall is more of a challenge than almost any of the other games in the franchise, and while initially, this heightened difficulty is used to contrast the explorable world, it only becomes a bother for the player. The twentieth time teleporting Dark Pirate Commandos forces you to encounter them, or the seventh long hallway wallowing through Rezbits or Darkling Tentacles that lock up navigation are trying to deal with and slow the game's pace to a crawl, especially as Dark Aether's empty nature only leaves these annoyances left to bug you.

{% image "/posts/2025/metroid-prime-2-echoes-1st-playthrough/dark_samus.jpg", "A low-health Dark Samus aims her weapon mid-air at Samus during a boss fight.", "Didn't do much but aurafarm.", "Metroid Recon." %}

Overall, I adored this game and rate it very highly amongst the rest of the *Metroid* franchise, the purple lunchboxes' already stacked library, and the search-action genre it uniquely takes a stand in along with the rest of the trilogy. There are several moments where *Echoes* feels like an evolution of the instant classic that precedes it, while many others serve as a playable reminder of how skilled Retro Studios is at making games in incredibly trying conditions, with emphasis on "reminder" and "trying." Future games in the series, particularly *Dread*, took the atmospheric and arsenal elements *Echoes* pioneered and ran with it to success that *Prime 2* barely missed out on achieving. Even still, I can't recommend this entry in what's probably my favorite franchise ever enough.
